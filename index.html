The pattern

Every leap in tools changed how we create.

Cave paintings. Written language. The printing press. Computers. Each one didn't just speed things up. It unlocked entirely new ways to think and communicate. We're at that moment again.

New tools don't just make old things faster. They make new things possible.

The computer isn't just a tool. It's a medium. And AI is the bridge between your ideas and the screen.

The Dynamic Medium

Designing with
the Dynamic Medium.

Think of it like a canvas, a sketchbook, a piece of clay. Code is a medium for exploring ideas, invoking feelings, shaping experiences. It responds to your intent in real time. That's what makes it dynamic.

"A dynamic medium doesn't just present ideas. It responds to them."

The problem

AI can build anything.
So why does everything look the same?

"Make it look good" gets you generic output every time.

Goals &
Constraints.

Constraints bind the space. Accessibility, Gestalt principles, UX laws, platform conventions. Goals define the direction. How you want the user to feel, what should delight them. Together they turn open-ended generation into focused, intentional design.

Creative direction

Design Goals

A director doesn't paint every frame. They set the intent. How should the user feel? What should delight them? Where should their attention land? Design goals turn vague ideas into clear creative direction.

When you give AI a goal, you stop asking for screens and start directing outcomes. Clarity of intent is the most powerful design tool you have.

Your brain automatically organizes visual information. Grouping, separating, prioritizing before you're even aware of it. These 9 principles are perceptual constraints. They define what the eye will do whether you plan for it or not.

Name them in your prompts and your layouts stop fighting the viewer's brain.

04

Constraints

UX Laws

These aren't opinions. They're research-backed constraints on human behavior. Fitts's Law, Hick's Law, Miller's Law. They tell you what works and what doesn't before a single user touches the product.

Use them as constraints in your prompts. The output gets measurably better.

01
Fitts's Law
The time to reach a target depends on its size and distance. Make important controls big and close.
02
Hick's Law
More choices = slower decisions. Use progressive disclosure. Show only what's needed right now.
03
Miller's Law
People can hold 7 ± 2 items in working memory. Chunk information into small, digestible groups.
04
Jakob's Law
Users spend most of their time on other sites. They expect your product to work the same way. Don't reinvent conventions.
05
Doherty Threshold
Responses under 400ms feel instant. Above that, users feel the wait. Speed is a design decision.
06
Peak-End Rule
People judge an experience by its most intense moment and how it ends. Not the average. Nail the peaks and endings.
07
Serial Position Effect
People remember the first and last items in a list best. Put your most important actions at the start or end of navigation.
08
Aesthetic-Usability Effect
Attractive designs are perceived as easier to use. Users tolerate minor usability issues when they love how something looks.
09
Tesler's Law
Every system has irreducible complexity. Simplifying the UI just moves complexity elsewhere. Usually to the developer or the system.
10
Von Restorff Effect
When multiple similar objects are present, the one that differs from the rest is most likely to be remembered. Contrast creates memory.
11
Zeigarnik Effect
People remember interrupted or incomplete tasks better than completed ones. Progress bars and incomplete states drive re-engagement.
12
Goal-Gradient Effect
People accelerate effort as they approach a goal. Showing progress (step 3 of 5) increases task completion rates significantly.
13
Recognition over Recall
It's easier to recognize something than to recall it from memory. Show options rather than asking users to remember commands or paths.
14
Postel's Law
Be liberal in what you accept, conservative in what you send. Design forms and inputs to handle messy, real-world user input gracefully.
15
Pareto Principle
80% of effects come from 20% of causes. Most users use a small subset of features. Optimize ruthlessly for the common case.
16
Law of Proximity
Objects near each other are perceived as related. Spacing is not decoration. It communicates structure and relationship.
17
Cognitive Load
Every element on screen costs mental effort to process. Reduce unnecessary complexity. If a user has to think, something can be simplified.
18
Occam's Razor
Among competing solutions, the simplest one is usually correct. When in doubt, remove. The best feature is the one you don't need to build.
19
Mental Models
Users approach your product with existing mental models from past experience. Design with those models in mind. Not against them.
20
Feedback Loops
Every action needs a reaction. Users need to know their input was received. Immediately, clearly, and without ambiguity. Silence is an error state.

📖 Design Vocabulary Glossary

Composition: The arrangement of visual elements to create a cohesive whole.

White Space: Empty area that gives elements room to breathe. Not wasted. Intentional.

Hierarchy: Visual priority. What the eye sees first, second, third.

Alignment: Elements sharing a common edge or centerline. Creates order.

Proximity: Related elements placed near each other. Distance implies relationship.

Contrast: Difference between elements. Size, color, weight. Contrast creates focus.

Balance: Visual weight distributed evenly (symmetrical) or intentionally offset (asymmetrical).

Rhythm: Repeated patterns of spacing, size, or color. Creates flow and consistency.

Typography: The art of arranging type. Font, size, weight, spacing, line height.

Component: A reusable UI building block. Buttons, cards, inputs, modals.

Token: A named design value (color, spacing, font size) that ensures consistency.

Progressive Disclosure: Showing only what's needed now. Reveal complexity as needed.

Affordance: A visual cue that suggests how to interact. A button looks pressable.

Delight: Small moments of joy. Animation, micro-interactions, unexpected polish.

Easing: How animation accelerates/decelerates. Ease-in, ease-out, bounce.

Anticipation: A brief motion before the main action. Prepares the viewer.

Visual Weight: How much attention an element demands. Size, color, density.

Grid: An invisible structure that aligns content. Columns, gutters, margins.

The Invisible Grammar
of Visual Design

by the design team

The Breath

When a designer talks about whitespace, they’re not describing empty space. They’re describing breathing room — the silence between notes that makes music meaningful. Apple’s product pages famously use whitespace to force your eye to rest, then look, then linger.

Negative space doesn’t just separate — it defines. The white around a headline is as responsible for that headline’s power as the type itself. Remove the breathing room and the words become wallpaper. Protect it, and the word becomes an event.

The Weight

Every element on a screen has visual mass. Typography is the densest of all — it carries meaning twice: once in what it says, once in how it looks. A headline set in a heavy serif commands attention before you’ve read a word. A body set in a light serif whispers.

Visual hierarchy is the invisible instruction set that tells your eye what to look at first, second, and third. Without it, every element screams at equal volume — and when everything demands attention, nothing gets it.

The Beat

There’s a rhythm to good design, as steady as a heartbeat. It comes from repeating spacing values, mirrored proportions, and the cadence of consistent sizing. When rhythm breaks — a card that’s 2px taller, a gap that doesn’t match the grid — something feels wrong even if a viewer can’t say why.

Contrast is the mechanism that makes rhythm visible. Not just light vs. Dark — but large vs. Small, quiet vs. Loud. Without contrast, there is no hierarchy. Without hierarchy, there is no story.

The Space

Every layout rests on a grid — an invisible scaffold of columns, rows, and gutters that most viewers will never consciously notice. The grid doesn’t constrain. It liberates. When you know where the walls are, you can decide which ones to push through.

The final act of any composition is restraint. It’s the decision not to add the element that seemed necessary — the whitespace you protect, the color you leave out. Good design isn’t about what’s present. It’s about what you had the discipline to leave behind.
